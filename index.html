Shihao Fu

I am a PhD candidate in the Faculty of Civil Engineering and Geosciences at Delft University of Technology, The Netherlands. My research focuses on the evolution of rock salt fracture geometry and mechanical properties during fluid–rock interactions, with applications to underground energy storage.

My PhD thesis investigates the mechanical–chemical evolution of rock salt fractures, combining flow-through visualization experiments, long-term healing experiments, and finite element modelling. I use laboratory measurements and image-based characterization to quantify dissolution and healing, and implement constitutive and cohesive-zone based approaches in ABAQUS and COMSOL to simulate fracture sealing and shear strength recovery.

Before joining TU Delft, I obtained my M.S. and B.S. degrees in Petroleum Engineering from China University of Petroleum (Beijing) and Yangtze University, respectively, where I worked on hydraulic fracture initiation and propagation in coal measure shale reservoirs.

Research

Sealing and healing behaviour of rock salt fractures

My current project studies how rock salt fractures dissolve, evolve, and heal under different stress and fluid conditions. Flow-through experiments are used to investigate the transition between diffusion- and advection-dominated regimes as a function of fluid concentration, flow rate, and initial fracture geometry. Image processing (MATLAB, Avizo, MeshLab) and surface reconstruction (SolidWorks) are applied to quantify changes in fracture morphology.

Long-term healing experiments (up to one year) are followed by direct shear tests to characterise shear strength recovery, internal friction angle, and stick–slip behaviour. On the modelling side, I extend an elastic–plastic constitutive model with healing behaviour in ABAQUS via user-defined Fortran subroutines and use COMSOL for reactive-transport simulations in complex fracture geometries.

Hydraulic fracturing in coal measure shale reservoirs

During my Master’s studies I performed true triaxial hydraulic fracturing experiments on 30 × 30 × 30 cm blocks from coal measure shale formations, investigating the influence of lithological layering and in-situ stress differences on fracture geometry. I developed a 3D hydraulic fracture model in ABAQUS using a coupled seepage–stress–damage framework with a cohesive zone representation of fractures.
